Attribution

A company may in many ways be likened to a human body. It has a brain and nerve centre which controls what it does. It also has hands which hold the tools and act in accordance with directions from the centre. Some of the people in the company are mere servants and agents who are nothing more than hands to do the work and cannot be said to represent the mind or will. Others are directors and managers who represent the directing mind and will of the company, and control what it does. The state of mind of these managers is the state of mind of the company and is treated by the law as such.

Denning LJ in H L Bolton (Engineering) Co Ltd v T J Graham and Sons Ltd [1957] 1 QB 159, 172

Attribution: contracts

  • Which ‘natural person’ represents the corporate person in each specific situation?
  • The articles will set out who can act on the company’s behalf in each situation: the members in general meeting or the directors
  • Who is authorised to sign contracts on the company’s behalf?
    • Contracts are made:
      • by a company, by writing under its common seal, or
      • on behalf of a company, by a person acting under its authority, express or implied. (CA 2006 s.43)
    • Documents are executed on a company’s behalf if signed by
      • two authorised signatories, or
      • a director of the company in the presence of a witness who attests the signature

Land registry: the common seal will normally be affixed to the deed in the presence of the company secretary and one director, or 2 directors, who attest the sealing by countersigning the deed and describing themselves by their respective offices of ‘secretary’ and ‘director’ or ‘director’ and ‘director’.

Authorised signatories

  • Who is an authorised signatory?
    • every director of the company (subject to articles), and
    • in the case of a private company with a secretary or a public company, the company secretary (CA 2006 s.44(3))
  • But what is “authority, express or implied”?

Ratification

  • If a person acts outside their actual authority as an agent, their act can be ratified by the company
    • If this happens then the person’s action becomes the company’s action
    • Ratification (as opposed to authorisation) happens afterwards

Attribution: liabilities

  • Vicarious liability
    • Harms caused by employees carried out during the course of their employment are carried over to the company
    • For discrimination; for deceit: beyond their acts as agents?
    • But when do they act as the company and when as ‘another person’?
  • Vicarious liability imposed when:
  1. the tort will have been committed as a result of activity being taken by the employee on behalf of the employer;
  2. the employee’s activity is likely to be part of the business activity of the employer; and
  3. the employer, by employing the employee to carry on the activity, will have created the risk of the tort committed by the employee.

Cox v Ministry of Justice [2016] UKSC 10, [2016] AC 660, 22

This extraordinary disease is a painful and invariably fatal cancer of the lining of the chest or of the abdomen. It is both slow growing (taking thirty years or more to appear) and virulent (killing within a year or so of first symptoms). Cape in the USA

  • North American Asbestos Corporation (NAAC): a wholly-owned subsidiary incorporated in 1953
    • Bringing asbestos mined in South Africa by Cape Asbestos South Africa – another subsidiary – to American markets
  • Cape was already well aware of the severe health risks posed by asbestos
    • Despite this NAAC’s plants, largely in poor Southern States continued to be badly ventilated and dusty
    • Workforce exposed to a substantial risk of developing various debilitating and often fatal diseases
  • Litigation

Separate Legal Personhood left intact while other areas of law pick up the pieces?

…a large proportion of the small firms created over the past twenty-five years in hazardous industries were created in order to limit tort liability.

Dearborn, M ‘Enterprise Liability: Reviewing and Revitalizing Liability for Corporate Groups’ (2009) 97 California Law Review 195, 198.

Question

Was Cape ‘present’ in the United States by virtue of its wholly-owned subsidiaries (such as NAAC) or through its close business links with a company called ‘Continental Productions Corporation’ (CPC) that marketed Cape products in America?



This would mean that there was no ‘veil’ between the subsidiary and Cape (its shareholder) and so liabilities could be inforced against Cape in the UK

(CPC was incorporated in Illinois in 1977. The shares in CPC were held by one Mr. Morgan, the President of NAAC.)

Three tests

  1. Were Cape and NAAC a single economic unit?

To the layman at least, the distinction between the case where a company itself trades in a foreign country and the case where it trades in a foreign country through a subsidiary, whose activities it has full power to control, may seem a slender one.

  1. Was NAAC a mere façade for Cape?

[Plaintiffs argued that Cape’s arrangements in America were] a device or sham or cloak for grave impropriety on the part of Cape or Capasco, namely to ostensibly remove their assets from the United States of America to avoid liability for asbestos claims whilst at the same time continuing to trade in asbestos there.

Adams and Others v Cape Industries Plc and Another [1990] Ch 443 (CA), 536-545

(\(\uparrow\) note the device/sham/cloak metaphors \(\uparrow\))

Three tests

  1. Was there an agency relationship between NAAC and Cape?

We accept that the intention of Cape in procuring the incorporation of N.A.A.C. in the State of Illinois was that N.A.A.C. should assist in the marketing of asbestos in the United States of America upon sales by Egnep or Casap to purchasers in the United States of America and that it was to be the marketing agent of the Cape group in the United States of America. .

Adams and Others v Cape Industries Plc and Another [1990] Ch 443 (CA), 536-545

Single Economic Unit?

If Cape had been an individual, it would not, in my view, have been arguable that in trading in such a fashion Cape had subjected itself to the territorial jurisdiction of the United States’ courts. Why should Cape’s corporate character justify any different conclusion?

Adams and Others v Cape Industries Plc. and Another”, 1990, 476

Mere façade?

C.P.C. was a company independently owned by Mr. Morgan and that the shares therein belonged to him in law and in equity. These findings by themselves make it very difficult to contend that the operation of C.P.C. involved a facade which entitles the court to pierce the corporate veil between C.P.C. and Cape/Capasco and treat them all as one. Is the legal position altered by the facts that Cape’s intention, in making the relevant arrangements (as we infer), was to enable sales of asbestos from the South African subsidiaries to be made while (a) reducing if not eliminating the appearance of any involvement therein of Cape or its subsidiaries, and (b) reducing by any lawful means available to it the risk of any subsidiary or of Cape as parent company being held liable for United States taxation or subject to the jurisdiction of the United States courts and the risk of any default judgment by such a court being held to be enforceable in this country? We think not.

Adams and Others v Cape Industries Plc. and Another”, 1990, 543-4

Agency?

It is indisputable that at very least a substantial part of the business carried on by N.A.A.C at all material times was in every sense its own business

Adams and Others v Cape Industries Plc. and Another”, 1990, 545

Expectations

Companies are entitled to expect that the Salomon rule will apply if they organise their affairs in group structures:

Save in cases which turn on the wording of particular statutes or contracts, the court is not free to disregard the principle of Salomon v Salomon & Co Ltd merely because it considers that justice so requires.

Adams and Others v Cape Industries Plc and Another [1990] Ch 443 (CA), 513

Pursuing liabilities without piercing the veil

I would emphatically reject any suggestion that this court is in any way concerned with what is usually referred to as piercing the corporate veil. A subsidiary and its company are separate entities. There is no imposition or assumption of responsibility by reason only that a company is the parent company of another company. The question is simply whether what the parent company did amounted to taking on a direct duty to the subsidiary’s employees.

Arden LJ, Chandler v Cape [2012] 1 WLR 3111, 3128

Corporate form as strategy

The capacity of MNEs to organise their legal form in such a way as to avoid responsibility for harm caused to the victims of such events is a cause for concern. In the context of corporate groups, the avoidance of responsibility can be achieved by interposing a separate legal entity between the victims and the ultimate controller of the group, be it a parent company or its controlling shareholders… Though in legal terms such devices are entirely normal and useful for the control of investment risks they are also likely to externalise other risks, in particular risk of harm, in morally and socially unacceptable ways.

Peter Muchlinski, ‘Limited liability and multinational enterprises: a case for reform?’ (2010) 34(5) Cambridge Journal of Economics 915, 916.
